Aug. 2023 - Mar. 2024

After I left the DMIR lab, I spent time in writing a “special paper” on the topic of how to learn the causal diagram, also as a summary for my research experience on “causal discovery” over the last two years.

As I was initially motivated by the popular causal science book (The Book of WHY), in this “special paper”, I attempted to interpret the topic by including more intuitions that are relative to causation in our lives. For example, notions of counterfactuals and intervention, the paradigm of human causal thinking, are introduced into two (simplified) applications of causal diagram in significant issues such as climate change and COVID-19.

The screens and the notes show that I was trying to understand the concept of “causal indirected effect”, and attempting to weave its intuitions into the story-telling in my paper. The reason why I choose this writing moment as a remembrance is that the “causal indirected effect” actually serves as a typical notion in the last chapter of The Book of WHY. I couldn’t understand it well when I start out learning causation by reading the book, but what delights me is that two years later I have really been doing professional research, and have the opportunity to review this interesting concept based on my own experience in causal science.

Specifically, the work was largely inspired by professional books as to causal discovery (e.g. Causation, Prediction, and Search [2001] (Peter Spirtes, Clark Glymour); Causality [2008] (Judea Pearl); Elements of Causal Inference [2017] (Jonas Peters, Bernhard Schölkopf, etc)). Hence, I hope to provide a “big picture” that clues to professional fields of causal diagram learning: the “down-stream” capability of a given causal diagram, and retrospectively the “upstream” task of how to learn the diagram — the “big picture” of the motivation in “causal discovery”.

Sep. 2021 - Aug. 2023

Data Mining and Information Retrieval Laboratory, DMIR lab

Research Assistant Intern (Advisors: Wei Chen，Ruichu Cai )

Starting in sophomore, I joined Prof. Ruichu Cai’s Artificial Intelligence lab as part of National Key R&D Programs in Causal Inference and Decision Theories. After reading a popular science book (The Book of WHY ) written by Prof. Judea Pearl — the winner of Turing Award and the director of Cognitive Systems Laboratory — my passion on causal science was immediately tapped on.

A picture of me and Prof. Ruichu Cai. The background is the 727-room (at top right, an unforgettable number for me), where I first participated the group-meeting as a special way of joining the DMIR lab, stumbling into the 727-room one afternoon in Sep. 2021.

Specifically, curious about how to discover a potential “causal structure” entailed by general data, I investigate the theories and developed the “hybrid-based” algorithms. Applying in fMRI brain data, our findings highlight that the non-linear interaction among brain regions’ structures can be inferred by statistics (e.g. probability and graphical models), drawing on the “mathematical causal asymmetry” of specific non-linear brain functions.
